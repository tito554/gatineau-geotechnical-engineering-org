Geotechnical Analysis for Soft Soil Tunnels in Gatineau

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

The contrast between Hull's compact till and Aylmer's deep marine clay deposits defines tunneling challenges in Gatineau. On one side, you hit dense glacial material within a few meters; on the other, you're dealing with 30 meters of Champlain Sea clay that deforms under its own weight. Our laboratory team processes undisturbed Shelby tube samples from both sectors, running consolidated-undrained triaxial tests to capture the undrained shear strength profile tunnel designers need. The difference in preconsolidation pressure between these two Gatineau neighborhoods can exceed 150 kPa, which directly affects face stability calculations. We've seen projects where the Atterberg limits on Aylmer samples showed plasticity indices above 40%, triggering additional consolidation testing before TBM selection could proceed. That's the kind of soil variability you face working in this city, and standard desktop assumptions don't cut it when you're 15 meters below Boulevard du Plateau.

Gatineau's Champlain Sea clays can lose 60% of their undrained shear strength when remolded, making undisturbed sampling and careful lab handling non-negotiable.

Methodology and scope

The triaxial cell sitting in our lab runs at back pressures up to 600 kPa, which is what you need to properly saturate Gatineau's stiff silty clays without disturbing the original structure. We mount the 50 mm diameter specimen, set the confining pressure to match the in-situ stress at tunnel depth, and let the pore pressure equalize overnight before shearing. For the soft Champlain Sea deposits under much of Gatineau, we typically run three specimens at different confining stresses to build a proper Mohr-Coulomb envelope. The data logger captures deviator stress, axial strain, and pore pressure every 0.1 seconds during the shear phase. When combined with oedometer consolidation curves, these parameters feed directly into PLAXIS or FLAC models that predict settlement troughs and lining loads. For projects near the Ottawa River where groundwater conditions fluctuate seasonally, we also recommend in-situ permeability testing to calibrate the hydraulic boundary conditions in the numerical model.

Local considerations

The Champlain Sea clay that underlies most of Gatineau's urban corridor is a Leda clay, deposited roughly 10,000 years ago in a post-glacial marine environment. Its salinity was leached out over millennia by freshwater infiltration, leaving a metastable flocculated structure that collapses when disturbed. In tunneling, this means face instability can propagate backward rapidly if support pressure drops even briefly. The sensitivity values we measure in the lab routinely exceed 30, classifying these deposits as quick clays under the Norwegian Geotechnical Institute's definition. A TBM open-face excavation through this material without real-time face pressure monitoring is a gamble. Our lab testing quantifies the remolded strength ratio so the contractor can set EPB pressures correctly. Combine this with Gatineau's Seismic Zone 2 classification under NBCC 2020, and the cyclic softening potential during an earthquake event becomes a parameter that must appear in the geotechnical baseline report—not an afterthought in the risk register.

How do you handle the sensitivity of Gatineau's Leda clays during testing?

The key is minimizing mechanical disturbance from the moment the Shelby tube leaves the borehole. We trim specimens in a humidity-controlled room using a wire saw, mount them without applying shear stress, and saturate under back pressure slowly to avoid collapsing the flocculated structure. We also run fall cone tests on trimmings to cross-check the remolded strength independently of the triaxial.

Which triaxial test type is most appropriate for tunneling in Champlain Sea clay?

For short-term stability during excavation, we recommend consolidated-undrained (CIU) tests with pore pressure measurement. This gives you the undrained shear strength profile and effective stress parameters in a single test series. If the tunnel lining is designed for long-term drained conditions, we add consolidated-drained (CID) tests or use the CIU effective stress envelope directly. The choice depends on whether your analysis is total stress or effective stress based.

How does NBCC 2020 seismic classification affect tunnel design in Gatineau?

Gatineau falls within a moderate seismic hazard zone, and the soft clay Site Class E or F amplification can increase spectral accelerations significantly. Our lab provides shear wave velocity profiles and cyclic triaxial data so the designer can assess degradation of soil stiffness and strength under seismic loading. For critical infrastructure, we also evaluate liquefaction susceptibility of any interbedded silt layers within the clay sequence, following NCEER/Youd-Idriss procedures adapted for fine-grained soils.
