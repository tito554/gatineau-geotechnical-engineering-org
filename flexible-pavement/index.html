Flexible Pavement Design & Geotechnical Support in Gatineau

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

Flexible pavement design in the Outaouais region requires a careful balance between structural capacity and resistance to our extreme freeze-thaw cycles. In Gatineau, the combination of wet fine-grained soils and long winter months creates a high risk for differential frost heave and spring load restrictions. The design process follows the AASHTO 1993 Guide for Design of Pavement Structures, supplemented by ASTM D1557 for laboratory compaction and ASTM D1883 for CBR determination. Rather than applying generic provincial tables, we extract undisturbed Shelby tube samples from the subgrade and run multi-point Proctor curves so the pavement section reflects actual field moisture-density relationships. For high-traffic corridors near Boulevard Maloney or the Rapibus transit lanes, we also incorporate resilient modulus estimates from CBR correlations to verify the structural number against projected ESALs over a 20-year design life. Before finalizing the granular base and asphalt concrete thickness, the subgrade stiffness measured in the CBR road test gives us the input modulus for the mechanistic-empirical layer analysis, while the Proctor tests define the compaction target for the contractor's quality control plan.

A subgrade CBR below 3% in Gatineau's Champlain Sea clays demands a minimum 900 mm of granular structure just to protect against frost penetration—anything less fails within two spring seasons.

Methodology and scope

In Gatineau we often see that imported granular borrow sources contain a higher percentage of fines than the specification allows, which directly impacts base course permeability and frost susceptibility. The technical workflow starts with a detailed grain-size distribution per ASTM D6913 on the proposed granular layers, followed by Atterberg limits on the natural subgrade to flag any plastic silts that would pump into the aggregate under cyclic loading. A typical project for a residential collector road involves compacting a 300 mm granular A base over a 600 mm granular B sub-base, but the exact thickness depends on the soaked CBR value measured after 96 hours of immersion. We run the compaction curve using modified Proctor energy because the heavier compaction equipment used by local contractors achieves densities close to that effort level. The asphalt concrete surface course thickness is then calculated from the structural number deficit after accounting for the granular layer coefficients. For subdivisions in the Aylmer plateau where bedrock is shallow, we recommend a separation geotextile and a slightly thicker sub-base to bridge over irregular rock surface without reflection cracking during the first winter.

Local considerations

The laboratory compaction hammer used for our Proctor curves is a 10 lb mechanical rammer dropping from 18 inches, five layers per mold, calibrated weekly against a reference steel block to maintain energy accuracy within 1%. When the subgrade CBR comes back below 2%, which happens occasionally in the old lacustrine deposits south of the Gatineau River, we shift the design strategy: instead of thickening the granular section endlessly, we evaluate chemical stabilization with lime or cement, running unconfined compression tests on treated specimens after 7 and 28 days of curing. The bigger operational risk in Gatineau is not the summer construction season—it's the early winter paving when the air temperature drops below 5°C and the contractor struggles to achieve compaction on the granular base before freeze-up. We always specify a winter shutdown window in the geotechnical report and recommend a proof-rolling with a loaded tandem ahead of asphalt placement to detect soft spots that would otherwise show up as alligator cracking after the first heavy rain.

What CBR value do you typically find in Gatineau's native subgrade soils?

It varies significantly by area. In the Hull sector and older parts of Gatineau near the Ottawa River, we often encounter Champlain Sea silty clays with soaked CBR values between 1.5% and 3%. Further north toward the Gatineau Hills, the glacial till subgrades are sandier and can yield CBR values of 8% to 15%, though occasional pockets of soft lacustrine clay still appear in low-lying areas.

Do you account for frost depth in the pavement design?

Yes. We use the freezing index for the Gatineau region, typically around 1,400 °C-days below freezing, to estimate the design frost penetration depth. The total pavement structure thickness—asphalt plus granular base and sub-base—must equal or exceed 60% to 80% of that depth depending on the subgrade frost susceptibility classification per USACE criteria. This prevents ice lens formation within the subgrade that causes differential heave during February and March.

What traffic data do you need to start the structural design?

We need the average annual daily traffic (AADT), the percentage of trucks, the design lane distribution factor, and the design life in years—typically 20 years for municipal roads in Gatineau. From this we calculate the cumulative 18-kip equivalent single axle loads (ESALs) and select the required structural number. If traffic counts are not available, we can estimate from the land use classification following the TAC Geometric Design Guide.
