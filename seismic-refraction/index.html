Seismic Tomography (Refraction/Reflection) in Gatineau: Bedrock Mapping & Velocity Models

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

Gatineau sits at elevations ranging from 40 meters near the Ottawa River to over 300 meters in the Gatineau Hills, a landscape carved into Paleozoic limestone and Precambrian Shield rock but blanketed by thick, sensitive marine clays from the post-glacial Champlain Sea. When a project in the Hull sector hits refusal on what seems like shallow bedrock, the real question is whether that refusal is competent limestone or a large erratic boulder floating in the clay—a distinction that only a velocity-based method can resolve. Seismic tomography provides that answer by generating a continuous 2D or 3D velocity model of the subsurface, mapping both the bedrock surface and the stiffness of the overlying soils for NBCC seismic site classification. On sites where the marine clay exceeds 15 meters thickness, combining the tomography results with a MASW survey often refines the VS30 profile and clarifies the site class boundary between Class D and Class E conditions.

Seismic tomography transforms a line of geophones into a continuous velocity cross-section, revealing whether that shallow refusal is competent limestone or just a glacial erratic.

Methodology and scope

Most Gatineau sites present a specific challenge: the velocity contrast between Champlain Sea clay (often 400–800 m/s) and the underlying limestone or gneiss (2500–5000 m/s) is dramatic, but the clay itself can be interbedded with silt and sand lenses that create misleading first arrivals in a simple refraction spread. We run high-resolution tomography lines with 4.5 Hz geophones at 2 to 3-meter spacing to capture these thin interbeds, and we process the data with wavepath eikonal tomography rather than straight-ray methods because the velocity gradients in the Gatineau overburden are too steep for basic assumptions. When reflection tomography is warranted—usually on deep infrastructure or tunnel feasibility studies—we deploy a 24-channel sledgehammer source array and record with a seismograph configured for common-midpoint stacking, which lets us image the bedrock surface beneath 30 meters of overburden with sub-meter accuracy. The resulting P-wave velocity sections are calibrated against borehole data wherever possible, and we deliver the model in formats compatible with both CAD platforms and geotechnical finite-element software, so the design team can import the geometry directly into their analysis.

Local considerations

Gatineau's development history traces a path from the low-lying industrial sectors of Hull to the residential expansion climbing the slopes of the Gatineau Hills—and the geotechnical risk follows that same trajectory. In the lower city, thick Champlain Sea clay deposits are prone to amplification during seismic events, and NBCC 2020 site classification demands a measured VS30, not a default assumption based on soil type alone. On the hillside lots of Chelsea and Cantley, the bedrock surface can plunge from 2 meters to 20 meters depth within a single building footprint, creating differential settlement risks that boreholes spaced 15 meters apart simply cannot catch. Seismic tomography fills that gap by providing a continuous bedrock profile, which lets the structural engineer place footings or piles with confidence that the bearing stratum is actually continuous. On major infrastructure corridors—the Rapibus transitway expansion being a recent example—reflection tomography has been used to map karst features in the limestone that could collapse under embankment loading, a hazard that isolated boreholes are notoriously poor at detecting.

Frequently asked questions

How deep can seismic refraction tomography image in Gatineau's geology?

With a 115-meter spread and a sledgehammer source, we typically achieve 25 to 35 meters of penetration in the Champlain Sea clay overburden. On sites where the clay is thinner and the bedrock is shallow limestone, the effective depth may be limited to 15 to 20 meters because the high-velocity rock refracts energy back to the surface quickly. For deeper targets—anything beyond 30 meters—we recommend reflection tomography or a hybrid approach that combines refraction for the overburden with a downhole seismic test to capture the rock mass velocity.

Can seismic tomography tell the difference between limestone and gneiss bedrock?

Yes, and this is particularly relevant in Gatineau where the boundary between the Paleozoic limestone platform and the Precambrian Shield runs roughly through the city. Limestone typically exhibits P-wave velocities in the 3000 to 4500 m/s range, while competent gneiss and granite of the Shield register 4500 to 6000 m/s. The tomography section shows this as a distinct velocity boundary, and when we calibrate against even one borehole, we can map the contact across the entire site with confidence.

How long does it take to get results from a seismic tomography survey?

Field acquisition for a typical two-line tomography survey in Gatineau takes one day with a two-person crew, assuming reasonable access. Data processing—including first-arrival picking, wavepath eikonal inversion, and generation of the final velocity sections—requires two to three additional business days. We deliver a preliminary bedrock profile within 48 hours of completing the field work so that the geotechnical team can proceed with foundation design, with the final report and digital data following by the end of the week.
