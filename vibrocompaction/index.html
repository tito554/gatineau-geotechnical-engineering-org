Vibrocompaction Design in Gatineau: Densification for Loose Granular Soils

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

A six-story residential project near Boulevard du Plateau hit refusal at 2.5 meters during standard compaction tests last fall. The culprit: a buried layer of loose fluvial sand deposited by ancient Ottawa River channels, a formation that appears across multiple Gatineau neighborhoods whenever excavation depths exceed the weathered crust. Deep densification was the only path forward, and vibrocompaction design became the critical engineering step before a single probe went into the ground. We modeled the target relative density at 70 to 85 percent, factoring in the water table at 3.4 meters and the proximity of existing row housing that limited allowable vibration amplitudes. For sites with finer interbedded layers, the CPT test provides the continuous tip resistance and friction ratio profiles needed to confirm that the vibro treatment has eliminated the loose pockets between the sand lenses.

Vibrocompaction transforms loose granular deposits into dense, load-bearing ground: the target is 70 to 85 percent relative density, verified by post-treatment CPT soundings at every probe location.

Approach and scope

The soil contrast between Gatineau's sectors is striking and dictates entirely different densification strategies. In the Hull sector near the river, clean medium sands with less than 5 percent fines respond quickly to vibratory energy: the probe advances under its own weight once the critical void ratio is breached, and we typically achieve 80 percent relative density within two passes. Move east to the older Aylmer terraces, and the picture changes: silty sands with 15 to 22 percent fines demand lower frequency operation and a backfill strategy calibrated to the fines content. Our design process maps the grain size envelope from laboratory data, then selects the vibrator power class (130 kW to 180 kW units for Gatineau's typical 8 to 14 meter treatment depths), probe spacing on a triangular grid, and the incremental lift thickness. When the natural ground includes organics or soft clay seams that won't densify, we integrate stone columns as a hybrid ground improvement solution that combines drainage and reinforcement with the vibratory compaction of the surrounding matrix.

Local ground factors

The mistake we see repeatedly in Gatineau's older infill developments is treating vibrocompaction as a cookbook process: same grid, same frequency, same duration regardless of what the CPT logs show. That approach fails when the stratigraphy changes over a few hundred meters, which it does constantly across the Hull and Plateau sectors. Undertreated zones leave loose pockets that settle differentially under foundation loads, cracking slab-on-grade floors and misaligning elevator shafts within the first two years. A second error is ignoring the vibration monitoring requirement when working within 15 meters of occupied structures: without triaxial geophones recording peak particle velocity at the nearest foundation, you risk cosmetic damage claims that delay occupancy permits. Our designs always include a vibration control plan with threshold limits referenced to CSA S832 and a pre-construction condition survey of adjacent buildings. Post-treatment verification via CPT at every probe location, supplemented by in-situ permeability tests when the densification must also reduce hydraulic conductivity for basement waterproofing performance, closes the quality assurance loop.

Common questions

What type of soil is suitable for vibrocompaction in Gatineau?

Vibrocompaction works best in clean sands and silty sands with fines content below 20 percent. Much of Gatineau's river terrace deposits in Hull and along the Gatineau River fall into this category. Sites with higher fines content or clay seams require a hybrid approach, often combining vibrocompaction with stone columns or preloading.

How long does a vibrocompaction design and execution take for a typical Gatineau site?

The design phase, including review of existing CPT data and preparation of the treatment plan, takes approximately two to three weeks. Field execution for a standard residential lot with 60 to 80 probe locations is typically completed in five to eight working days, followed by CPT verification within 48 hours of treatment.

What is the cost range for vibrocompaction design in Gatineau?

The engineering design package for vibrocompaction in Gatineau typically ranges from CA$1,900 to CA$7,460, depending on the treated area, number of probe locations, and the complexity of the vibration monitoring plan required for adjacent structures.

How do you verify that the ground has been adequately densified?

We perform CPT soundings at every probe location after treatment, comparing the cone tip resistance and sleeve friction profiles against the target values defined in the design. The acceptance criterion is achieving the specified relative density (typically 70 to 85 percent) throughout the treatment depth. Additional verification may include in-situ permeability tests when the densification also serves a waterproofing function.
