Field Permeability Testing in Gatineau (Lefranc & Lugeon Methods)

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

A recent excavation for a mid-rise residential project along Boulevard du Mont-Bleu hit a perched water table at just 4.2 meters, a scenario not uncommon in Gatineau where the interface between granular overburden and fractured bedrock creates unpredictable groundwater flow. The geotechnical design team needed a reliable in-situ hydraulic conductivity value to size the permanent dewatering system, and lab tests on disturbed samples were not going to cut it. We deployed a variable-head Lefranc test in two boreholes to isolate the sandy silt layer, yielding field-scale permeability data that changed the pump specification entirely. In the Canadian Shield context, where rock mass discontinuities control flow, combining the Lefranc method with a Lugeon test in the underlying limestone provides a complete hydrogeological profile that lab permeameters simply cannot replicate.

A five-stage Lugeon test in Gatineau limestone typically reveals transmissivity variations of two orders of magnitude within a single 3-meter packer interval.

Methodology and scope

Gatineau sits at the boundary of the St. Lawrence Lowlands and the Precambrian Shield, meaning subsurface conditions can shift from deep marine clays in the Hull sector to shallow, fractured bedrock in Aylmer and Chelsea within a single project footprint. The Lefranc test, run as a falling-head or constant-head test in a cased borehole, measures hydraulic conductivity in the 10^-5 to 10^-7 m/s range typical of Gatineau's silty till and varved clay deposits. For the crystalline limestone and gneiss formations that dominate the northern part of the city, the Lugeon test applies a pressurized water injection in five-stage cycles up to 10 bars, capturing the hydraulic aperture of joints and bedding planes. Our field teams follow ASTM D4630 for rock mass testing and record pressure-flow curves using digital transducers, logging every litre per minute against packer inflation pressure. A single test interval can reveal whether a rock mass needs contact grouting, as we found during a slope stability assessment near the Gatineau River where open joints were transmitting water directly into a planned cut face.

Local considerations

The Hull district and the Plateau sector sit on radically different hydrogeological regimes despite being only eight kilometres apart. Hull's deep Champlain Sea clays show k values below 1×10^-8 m/s, meaning drainage is negligible and excavation stability relies on pore pressure dissipation measured via piezometers. Contrast this with the Plateau neighbourhood where thin till over fractured limestone produces Lugeon values above 30 Lu, requiring pre-excavation grouting to control inflow into underground parking structures. The biggest risk we encounter in Gatineau is assuming a single permeability value across a multi-block project site. A Lefranc test in the overburden may indicate low permeability, but an untested rockhead transition zone can deliver concentrated flow paths that destabilize a foundation during spring thaw. We have seen this exact failure mode on a commercial project near the Ruisseau de la Brasserie, where a design based solely on clay permeability led to a 72-hour dewatering emergency during footing construction. Targeted in-situ testing across each hydrostratigraphic unit is not optional here.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between a Lefranc test and a Lugeon test?

The Lefranc test measures hydraulic conductivity in soil by adding or removing water in a cased borehole and recording the water level change over time. It is used in granular and fine-grained soils. The Lugeon test is a pressurized water injection test performed in rock, using a packer to isolate a section of borehole. It measures the water take in litres per minute per metre of test interval at increasing pressure steps, giving a Lugeon value that reflects fracture aperture and connectivity.

How much does a field permeability test cost in Gatineau?

A single Lefranc or Lugeon test in Gatineau typically ranges from CA$930 to CA$1,410, depending on depth, number of test intervals, and whether the borehole is already available or requires drilling. A complete campaign with multiple intervals, packer setup, and reporting will be quoted based on site-specific logistics.

When is a Lugeon test required instead of a Lefranc test?

A Lugeon test is specified when the project involves rock excavation, dam foundations, grouting design, or deep tunnels where fracture flow dominates. In Gatineau, any excavation that intercepts the limestone or gneiss bedrock beneath the overburden should include Lugeon testing. The Lefranc test is appropriate for soil layers where granular permeability controls groundwater movement.

How long does an in-situ permeability test take in the field?

A single Lefranc test can be completed in 45 to 90 minutes once the borehole is prepared. A full five-stage Lugeon test typically takes 90 to 150 minutes per interval, including packer inflation, pressure stabilization at each stage, and recovery monitoring. For a typical Gatineau project with two to three test intervals, field work is usually completed within one day.
