Methodology and scope

A warehouse extension off Rue de l'Aéroport last November showed exactly why Gatineau demands a different approach. The contractor placed a 230 mm slab over a well-graded granular base, but the real challenge was the joint layout. With winter temperatures dropping to minus 30 degrees Celsius and summer highs above 30, the concrete panels move more than the textbook predicts. We specified dowel bars at every contraction joint and widened the isolation joints where the slab meets the loading dock walls. The mix itself used Type GU cement with a water-cement ratio of 0.40 and an air-entrainment target of 6 percent. Our lab ran freeze-thaw resistance cycles per ASTM C666 and flexural strength beams per ASTM C78 to confirm the 4.5 MPa modulus of rupture before the pour. For projects on soft alluvial soils near the Rivière des Outaouais, we recommend an in-situ permeability test to verify drainage coefficients used in the thickness design. The key takeaway from the airport job: curing compound application within 15 minutes of finishing makes the difference between a 30-year slab and one that spalls in five seasons.

Local considerations

Gatineau's urban expansion through the 1970s and 1980s pushed development onto the Champlain Sea clay plain, a geological formation that still dictates foundation and pavement performance today. The biggest risk with rigid pavement on these soils is differential heave. When one slab corner lifts 40 millimeters during a January freeze while the adjacent corner stays put, the resulting faulting creates a ride quality problem that no diamond grinding can permanently fix. The second major risk is joint deterioration from de-icing salts. Gatineau applies over 130,000 tonnes of road salt annually across the region, and the chloride-laden meltwater attacks the dowel bars and the concrete matrix at the joint edges. Without epoxy-coated dowels and a well-sealed joint reservoir, the corrosion products expand and spall the concrete within three to five years. We address these risks in the laboratory phase by specifying low-permeability concrete mixes and testing aggregate durability under saturated conditions. The field solution involves strict subgrade moisture control during construction and a proof-rolling pass before the base course goes down.

Frequently asked questions

What thickness of rigid pavement is typical for a commercial parking lot in Gatineau?

For a standard commercial parking lot with passenger vehicles and occasional delivery trucks, we typically specify 150 to 180 mm of concrete over a 150 mm granular base. If the lot serves heavy truck traffic or a waste collection area, the slab thickness increases to 200–230 mm. The final number depends on the subgrade k-value we measure on site and the expected traffic loading over the design life.

How do you prevent cracking from Gatineau's freeze-thaw cycles?

Cracking control combines three strategies: a properly designed air-entrainment system (5–7% air voids), a low water-cement ratio (0.40–0.45 maximum), and a joint spacing that respects the slab's thermal movement. We also specify wet curing or a membrane-forming curing compound applied immediately after finishing. The air-void spacing factor measured under ASTM C457 should be below 0.20 mm for durable performance.

What is the cost range for rigid pavement design services in Gatineau?

Our rigid pavement design services in Gatineau typically range from CA$2,450 to CA$7,770, depending on the project scope. A basic design for a small commercial lot with standard mix testing falls at the lower end, while a full package for an industrial facility or roadway with subgrade evaluation, multiple mix trials, and joint detailing is at the upper end of the range.

How long does the concrete mix design and testing process take?

The laboratory phase takes approximately four to five weeks from aggregate sampling to final mix approval. Week one covers aggregate characterization: gradation, specific gravity, and absorption. Weeks two and three involve trial batching and casting test specimens. The 28-day compressive and flexural strength breaks happen in weeks four and five. Freeze-thaw durability testing runs concurrently and may extend slightly beyond the 28-day window.

Do you test the subgrade before designing the rigid pavement?

Yes, subgrade evaluation is mandatory in our process. We perform in-situ plate load tests to determine the modulus of subgrade reaction (k-value), which directly feeds into the thickness design. On Gatineau's Champlain Sea clays, we also check the moisture content and compaction level at the top of the subgrade. If the soil is too wet or too soft, we recommend a thicker base course or a stabilization treatment before the concrete goes down.
