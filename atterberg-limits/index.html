Atterberg Limits Testing in Gatineau – Clay Plasticity & Shrink-Swell Evaluation

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

ASTM D4318 defines the protocol, but in Gatineau the stakes are tied directly to the Champlain Sea clay that underlies much of the Outaouais region. This post-glacial deposit is notorious for its high sensitivity and metastable structure: the liquid limit can exceed 60% and the plasticity index frequently lands above 30, placing the material squarely in the CH (high-plasticity clay) category. When the natural water content approaches the liquid limit — a condition we encounter regularly in Aylmer and Hull sectors — even modest loading can trigger remolding and a dramatic loss of shear strength. The grain-size distribution helps us bracket the silt fraction that often accompanies these clays, but the Atterberg limits provide the direct measure of how the material will behave through seasonal wet-dry cycles. For projects within the City of Gatineau’s jurisdiction, Part 4 of the Quebec Construction Code and CSA A23.3 require plasticity-based classification before foundation design can proceed, and our laboratory delivers results typically within three business days of sample receipt.

A liquidity index above 1.0 in Gatineau’s Champlain Sea clay is a direct indicator that the soil will behave as a viscous fluid under remolding — excavation support becomes mandatory, not optional.

Methodology and scope

The surficial geology across Gatineau shifts from thick Champlain Sea silty clays north of the Autoroute 50 corridor to coarser fluvial deposits near the Ottawa River shoreline, and this variability shows up immediately in the plasticity data. We have measured liquid limits ranging from 25% in the sandy clay loams found east of the Gatineau River to over 75% in the soft lacustrine clays encountered at depths of 4 to 8 metres beneath the Plateau district. The plasticity index in these deeper clays regularly sits between 35 and 50, which puts them in the high-expansion category according to both the USBR classification and the CHBA’s moisture-sensitivity guidelines. Our lab runs Casagrande cup and thread-rolling procedures on the minus 425 µm fraction of each sample, and we report the results alongside the natural water content so that the liquidity index can be calculated directly. For sites where the clay shows a liquidity index above 0.8, we typically recommend supplemental in-situ testing — CPT testing provides a continuous profile of undrained shear strength without disturbing the fabric that the Atterberg limits have already characterized. When the project involves cut slopes or retaining structures, pairing the plasticity data with a slope stability analysis under drained and undrained conditions is standard practice in Gatineau’s geotechnical reports.

Local ground factors

The risk profile for a foundation in the Hull sector versus one in the newer developments east of Boulevard Lorrain illustrates why plasticity testing cannot be treated as a checkbox exercise. Hull sits on deep, compressible clay that has been consolidating for thousands of years but still shows a preconsolidation pressure that is easily exceeded by a four-storey structure; the plasticity index there averages 40, and the clay fraction exceeds 60%, meaning the soil is highly active and will shrink and swell with seasonal moisture changes. East of the Gatineau River, we find more interbedded sands and silts where the liquid limit drops below 30 and the plasticity index hovers around 8 to 12 — these soils classify as CL or ML and present far less volume-change hazard. The real danger in Gatineau is the intermediate case: a 2-metre clay layer with a PI of 25 buried beneath a metre of sand, which looks benign in a test pit but will still generate differential heave under a lightly loaded slab-on-grade. Combining Atterberg limits with Proctor compaction testing lets us assess whether the on-site clay can even be reused as engineered fill, or if it must be undercut and replaced with granular material to meet the 95% modified Proctor density specified in most Gatineau geotechnical reports.

Common questions

What do Atterberg limits actually tell me about my Gatineau building site?

They define the water-content boundaries between the solid, semi-solid, plastic, and liquid states of the fine-grained soil on your site. In Gatineau, where Champlain Sea clay dominates, a high liquid limit and plasticity index mean the soil will shrink when it dries and swell when it wets — and it will lose most of its strength if it gets saturated and remolded during excavation. This data feeds directly into foundation depth decisions, slab-on-grade design, and whether you need to remove and replace the native clay.

How much does Atterberg limits testing cost for a typical Gatineau project?

For a standard multi-point Atterberg determination on a single sample, expect a cost in the range of CA$80 to CA$120. The total depends on the number of samples, whether we are running one-point or multi-point liquid limits, and the turnaround time you need. We can provide a firm quote once we know how many soil horizons you need characterized.

How many samples do I need for a geotechnical investigation in Gatineau?

We recommend at least one Atterberg determination per distinct soil horizon encountered in the borehole or test pit. In Gatineau, that often means a sample at 1.5 to 2 metres depth where the weathered crust ends, another at 4 to 6 metres in the intact Champlain Sea clay, and potentially a third if you hit a sand or silt interbed. For a single-family home, three to four samples usually provide adequate coverage.

Can you run Atterberg limits on the same day we deliver the samples?

Same-day results are possible with our rush service, but the standard protocol requires air-drying the sample, which typically adds a day. For most Gatineau projects, we deliver the full report within two to three business days. If you are on a tight excavation schedule, let us know when you submit the samples and we will prioritize the work.

Does the Quebec Construction Code require Atterberg limits for foundation design?

Yes — Part 4 of the Quebec Construction Code, which references CSA A23.3 and the National Building Code of Canada, requires that the bearing stratum be classified by its plasticity characteristics when fine-grained soils are present. In Gatineau, where expansive clays are widespread, the code effectively mandates Atterberg limits as part of the geotechnical investigation for any permanent structure.
