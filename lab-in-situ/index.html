In-Situ Testing in Gatineau

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

In Gatineau, in-situ testing is essential for characterizing the complex Champlain Sea clay deposits and glacial till that dominate the region. These field investigations directly measure soil behavior without disturbance, ensuring designs comply with the National Building Code of Canada and CAN/BNQ 2501-135 for geotechnical site investigations. A fundamental component is the field density test (sand cone method), which verifies compaction levels in engineered fills, an absolute necessity given the area's sensitive silty clays prone to settlement.

This testing supports a wide range of projects, from residential foundation inspections on Gatineau's hillside lots to municipal roadway embankments. The data collected provides critical parameters for bearing capacity and settlement analyses, directly informing safe and economical foundation designs. Accurate in-situ measurements are the only reliable way to confirm that ground improvement and compaction efforts meet the strict specifications required for long-term structural stability in this unique geological setting.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between an active and a passive anchor?

An active anchor is prestressed after installation: we apply a jacking load and lock it off against the wall, which actively compresses the retained soil and minimizes movement before excavation proceeds. A passive anchor is not prestressed; it only develops resistance when the wall starts to deflect and the anchor stretches. In Gatineau's soft clay, we typically specify active anchors for the upper rows of a shoring system to limit wall deflection, and passive hollow-bar anchors for the lower rows where some movement is acceptable and rapid installation is a priority.

How do you account for the Champlain Sea clay in anchor design?

Champlain Sea clay poses two specific challenges: low shear strength in the bond zone and time-dependent creep under sustained load. We address the first by extending the bond length into the underlying glacial till or bedrock whenever possible. If the anchor must be bonded in clay, we use a larger diameter drill hole and a post-grouting program to densify the interface. For creep, we limit the bond stress to a fraction of the ultimate value based on creep test data, following the PTI DC-35.1 approach. We also specify a 60-minute extended creep test for production anchors in clay to verify the lock-off load won't cause long-term relaxation.

Do you handle the installation, or just the design?

We provide the anchor design, sealed shop drawings, and field testing supervision. The physical installation — drilling, grouting, and stressing — is performed by qualified anchor drilling contractors. We work with several local Gatineau drillers and can coordinate the entire package, but we maintain an arm's-length relationship with the contractor to ensure unbiased test interpretation. This design-build oversight model is standard practice under PTI DC-35.1 and gives the owner confidence that the installed anchors meet the design intent.
